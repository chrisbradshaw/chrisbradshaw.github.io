El Diablo Taco Truck

Greenpoint, Brooklyn

When you're in a band you quickly become friends with bartenders, which is how the members of Ex Hex — former Helium and Wild Flag guitarist/vocalist Mary Timony, guitarist Betsy Wright and drummer Laura Harris — end up on a back patio enjoying tacos and guacamole served from the El Diablo food truck behind them. The band has an off-day in New York City, and as we sit down to discuss the grueling schedule of a touring musician, you can hear the passion in their voices. "We're so used to carrying around our own equipment and we aren't today,” Harris playfully explains between bites, “so maybe this is what Metallica feels like. Sometimes I dream someone will set my drums up for me and all I have to do is play."

After filling up on El Diablo’s tacos out back, Ex Hex heads inside Union Pool. “When we're not on tour I work at a music venue,” Harris says when asked how she learns about new music, “so I end up seeing a lot of bands through that. I just saw Flag and they were amazing. That was really inspiring to see.” The Brooklyn bar and music venue is the perfect place to refresh their drinks and attempt to freeze time in a retro photo booth. Behind the curtain, you can hear the band laughing and coordinating poses right as the camera flashes. About 10 minutes later, the booth dispenses a four-frame strip of photos, immortalizing a handful of carefree moments that touring bands have come to appreciate.

Russell St. Recording

GREENPOINT, BROOKLYN

Feeling a rush of creativity, the trio head to Russell Street Recording, a nondescript studio located in Greenpoint, where the members of Ex Hex pick up various instruments and start riffing as they kick around song ideas. “Before we recorded the first record we practiced almost everyday,” Wright explains. “The only thing that changed in the studio was how we wanted the mics to be placed to get the right vibe for the production.” The “right vibe” is a notion that seems to come easy for these rockers as the session ends with an impromptu jam of Bob Marley's reggae classic “Stir It Up.” They laugh when asked if Ex Hex could be moving into a more reggae direction with their next album, but the truth is that it sounds pretty good.

Baby's All Right

Williamsburg, Brooklyn

It’s nearly 8:30 as Ex Hex tucks away the equipment and gears up to watch a live show at notorious Brooklyn venue Baby's All Right. With its dark wood and low lighting, it’s the ideal spot for the trio to transition into the evening. The minute they walk through the door the girls kick up the heels of their Chuck II Shield Canvas’ knowing that hours of dancing and spilled drinks will not lead to a night of soggy shoes, thanks to the Lunarlon cushioning and water resistant shield canvas.

The music fades out, the crowd roars, and you can feel the air of nostalgia settle over the band. They shuffle out reminiscing on the show they performed there early on in their career.

Yeah Shanghai Deluxe

Chinatown, New York

Ex Hex end the night in Chinatown, sampling the Asian cuisine that has made this neon-illuminated neighborhood so iconic, finally sitting down after spending the day traversing the city. “You just have to be okay with telling people that you aren't super available a lot of the time,” Wright says when asked how the members of Ex Hex manage to balance their jobs, careers and personal life when they’re on the road so much.

“We had a joke on tour that every time we would pass a chicken factory or something like that we would say, ‘at least we don't work there,'" Harris chimes in, adding that as grueling as things can get on the road, Ex Hex are still thankful to be living their dream.

With that, the trio finish their dumplings, tighten their laces, and head back out into the night not knowing what other adventures will be awaiting them in the next city.

The Thing

Greenpoint, Brooklyn

The funk is thick at The Thing—the Greenpoint institution that is equal parts thrift shop and record emporium—and that’s exactly how White Lung likes it. The punk rock foursome is busy thumbing through mountains of unorganized records while figuring out if they really need that portable grill for the tour van. It’s a sticky, humid day in Brooklyn, making The Thing’s close quarters that much more claustrophobic. But soon that band will be back in the van heading north to the upper edge of the borough.

White Lung are taking a well-earned break from an unforgiving tour schedule in support of their stellar album Paradise. Looking road-worn but glamorous, the foursome make a pit stop at Brooklyn’s punk-metal Mecca, St.Vitus in Greenpoint. The venue is brimming with leather-clad metalheads, hanging out and trading stories against a backdrop of posters featuring rock icons like Motörhead’s Lemmy Kilmister. Naturally, there is an insanely loud soundcheck happening onstage—you’d figure a band on break from tour might want a break from the noise, but White Lung take a seat and enthusiastically talk about the scene, music, and what makes White Lung tick.

“Everyone here has their own process,” adds guitarist Kenneth William. “There aren’t any surprises. No one is bringing in a set of bagpipes or a harp or something. Everyone has this picture in their heads of four dudes in a garage getting together and making a classic record, but that’s not how it works. We can do great work separately because we trust each other.”

The sky is threatening rain as the band ducks out of St. Vitus and heads to Milk and Roses—a bar and brunch spot— a block South. It is quiet—a sort of palate cleanser after the hard-charging sonic assault in St.Vitus.

East River Ferry

Brooklyn to Manhattan

From the calm backdrop of Milk and Roses they head to the East River Ferry where a light drizzle starts to fall. (Rain and waterways are a welcome reminder of home for the Vancouver-based rockers.) The ferry is rocking in the chop but after playing packed shows on grimy, sweat-stained stages all over the world, the bandmates have developed great balance. Those early tours were formative years for White Lung, and it created a special sort of trust between the members that comes through on Paradise. “We wanted to give everyone their space to work,” lead singer Mish Way told SPIN. “There isn’t a need to throw everything at each other, we trust each other to do good work and everyone has their part”

Saint Marks Place

East Village, New York

The band hops into a cab on Manhattan’s east side and speeds downtown along 2nd Avenue towards the East Village. The drizzle has turned into a downpour, but the foursome hops out of their cab and makes their way down neon-flanked St. Mark’s Place. They’re peeking into the Japanese restaurants lining the block and scoping out what has defined the New York punk territory for decades. “This is a break from our routine,” Way says, “and touring is all about routine. I’ve nailed down my vocal warm-ups so I can handle all 14 exercises in 15 minutes. A good show is all about being prepared.” But tonight they’re putting those drills on hold.

There’s no show and roaring crowd tonight, and the band is free to go wherever their feet will take them.

Nick's Apartment

Midtown, New York

The only time you’ll see Nick Catchdubs, co-founder of Fool’s Gold records, stop to catch his breath is in front of a turntable. Catchdubs seems at peace in watching vinyl spin at 33 rpm and it lets some peace into his hectic daily schedule. “For those good days I just want to get everything handled that I need to,” he told SPIN in his Manhattan apartment. “When my mind is clear and I’m not stressed the end product is always a lot better.”

From his home in Manhattan, Catchdubs makes a pit stop at St. Mark’s Comics for some creative refresh before he heads to the offices of Fool’s Gold, the flourishing New York record label he founded with A-Trak back in 2007. After 20-minutes of flipping through comic classics, Catchdubs is ready to make the trek to Brooklyn and check-in with the minds behind the magic at Fool’s Gold.

SUNSHINE PINBALL BAR

GREENPOINT, BROOKLYN

Catchdubs ducks out of the Summer shower and into Sunshine Laundromat where he’s surrounded by the old-school kitsch of the dozens of vintage pinball machines lining the bar’s walls. “I’m starting to think of my music in visual terms,” he reveals. “I want to channel — like what would the X-Men jam be? What about the soundtrack to some vintage sci-fi? I want to get back that feeling I had when I was a kid reading comics.”

Catchdubs wraps up his last game and heads to the 5th floor of a nondescript office commercial building. This is the Space Pit, and it’s where Catchdubs has spent more late night hours than he can count. “This is where I can translate all those ideas into reality. Once I get in here it makes it all real.” He sits down, pops open his laptop, and takes a moment to breathe deep before diving back into work.
